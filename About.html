ALABAT

QUEZON, PHILIPPINES

History of Alabat

Alabat, officially the Municipality of Alabat (Tagalog: Bayan ng Alabat), is a municipality in the province of Quezon, Philippines. According to the 2024 census, it has a population of 15,744 people.

The town is home to a few speakers of the critically endangered Inagta Alabat language, one of the most endangered languages in the world as listed by UNESCO.

Founded in the year 1882 by a Spanish priest from Gumaca, the town originally built an ermita that honours Our Lady of Monte Carmelo.

Its first name was Gordo, which means "fat", due to the island's shape. It was changed to Gordon, Barcelona, and later on Alabat. The formal establishment of the town occurred in the year 1900.

Etymology

Before Spanish colonization, the mountains were already inhabited by the “Baluga” (aborigines). Nomadic by nature, they would clean patches of land, plant rice and vegetables and hunt.

Local folklore says that a Spanish priest from the mainland saw the Island from across Lamon Bay. He crossed the bay on a boat and founded a church at what is today barangay Gordon.

The name Alabat came from the word Alâbât (local Tagalog word for balustrade or balcony).

Another local folklore says that the name 'Alabat' was from Muslim origins where Alabat comes from Allah-bat, and if the word is mixed up actually means Bathala, the local word for god.
